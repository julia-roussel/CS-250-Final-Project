Interpreting user needs means turning user stories into actionable and clear requirements. Users typically describe problems vaguely or in a solution focused way. The goal of the team is to ask questions and find the real goal, then turn it into a feature that can be delivered. User Stories are also useful for situations like this; they help by keeping the development focused on the outcomes. Also helps make it easier to break work into manageable tasks and track if task acceptance criteria have been met. 

When approaching developing programs, think in terms of iteration rather than building everything at once. Agile methods like Scrum, support iterative practices: define the functionality, design a solution, implement it, test early and refine it. This is done through sprints, backlogs, and feedback, helping the team stay organized and adaptable. 

Being a good team member in software development is more than just writing code. It means, communicating clearly, meeting commitments and writing clear code. This involves accountability, communicating when something is unclear or bringing up blockers, allowing for open dialog between all parts of the team. Good team mates often participate in discussions, assist teammates, and respect the collective process. It often involves collaborating, contributing to the overall success of the team and giving and receiving feedback. In Agile environments, adaptability and collaboration are just as important as technical skills, a team’s success rely on these factors.
